Am I Ready to Buy a Home?

Determining whether you are ready to purchase your first home is an important undertaking, and there are several factors to consider when deciding if you’re ready:

  • • Are you going to be in the house for at least five years? Make sure your job and family life are in a settled state. If you move prior to year five of occupancy, it may not be the best financial decision considering Realtor commissions, attorney’s fees, and taxes you would be responsible for paying.
  • • Are you ready to tackle day-to-day home maintenance responsibilities? Unlike renting, when you own your own home, you are responsible for handling all repairs and maintenance. Make sure there is extra room in your budget and savings for unexpected expenses like a new HVAC, new appliances, and roof repairs.
  • • Have you saved enough money for a down payment? The down payment is usually what stands between future homeowners and their dream of homeownership. Banks and lending institutions are looking for you to have some skin in the game before they loan you a large sum of money. At least 3% down is generally required to be approved for a mortgage and more than that is ideal. Depending on your vocation, income, and where you live, grants may also be an option to subsidize or completely cover this expense. Gifts from family members are also a common source of a down payment—just make sure to talk to your mortgage consultant about any gifts you may be receiving.
  • • Do you have money set aside separate of the down payment funds for closing costs and financial emergencies? Once the down payment and closing costs are accounted for, it is also imperative that you have separate emergency fund—we usually recommend between three months and six months of living expenses. If you are short on closing costs, ask your real estate agent about negotiating for the seller to pay them.
Should I Buy or Keep Renting?

Deciding when it’s the right time to buy a home is one of the most important decisions you’ll ever make. There are so many factors to consider including timing and the state of your finances. Carefully consider the factors below before deciding to stop renting and leaping into homeownership:

  • • Are you planning on staying in the same location for at least the next five years? There are expenses involved in selling a home (taxes, realtor fees, closing costs, etc.). You could potentially lose money if you move too soon. Make sure you are happy and settled in your job and will not be relocating anytime soon. Also, if you have school-aged children or children who will become school-age within five years, make sure you like the schools where you plan on buying.
  • • Do you have room in your budget to cover extra expenses associated with homeownership? When you buy a house, you will be responsible for home maintenance, repairs, and updates. So, you need to make sure extra expenses fit into your budget.
  • • Have you put aside money for a down payment and closing costs? The down payment is the main barrier between most people and their dream of owning a home. In general, you need to have at least 3 to 3.5 percent of the total home purchase price for a home loan approval. However, 5 to 10 percent is ideal. Twenty percent down is required to avoid mortgage premium insurance. Keep in mind, many first-time homebuyers utilize a gift from a family member to help with the down payment. Talk with your mortgage consultant if you anticipate a gift from a family member. Also, ask your loan officer if there are any grant programs in your area to help cover your down payment. Closing costs generally range from 3-5% of the house’s purchase price. The seller often will pay for closing costs, but that’s not always the case. So, talk to your realtor if you need additional help with closing funds.
Buying vs. Renting

There are benefits to buying a house and renting from a landlord. It’s up to you which makes the most sense for your situation.

Pros of Buying
  • Personalize the home with your own color palette, landscape design, and style upgrades.
  • No pet restrictions (unless you are also governed by a condo board).
  • Earn equity if your home appreciates in value.
  • Write-off the interest part of your home loan on your taxes.
Pros of Renting
  • Freedom to relocate to a new city.
  • Landlord is responsible for home repairs, maintenance, and sometimes yardwork.
  • Doesn’t require saving a large sum for a down payment.
  • Refundable deposit when you move out.
