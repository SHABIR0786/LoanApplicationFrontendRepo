When Should I Consider a Mortgage Refinance?

Before deciding whether to refinance, think about what you want to accomplish. In general, people decide to refinance to take cash out, shorten their loan term, or lower their monthly payment. Sometimes you might want to remove a co-borrower from your loan, especially in the case of divorce. You need to be living in your home and have a good payment history for at least one year before refinancing is an option. Now, let’s explore each reason in more detail.

Get a Cash Out Refinance

A cash out refinance allows you to use the equity in your home any way you like. The most common ways people use the cash include the following:

  • • Pay off higher interest credit cards
  • • Use for higher education expenses
  • • Pay for home improvements or renovations
  • • Pay off car or student loan debt

Using a mortgage loan to finance these expenses or payoffs is savvy because the interest rates on mortgages are usually much lower than that of other loans and credit cards. The interest on mortgage loans is also tax deductible. Cash out refinance mortgage rates are slightly higher than purchase and other refinance loans.

The amount you can take out will depend on the current value of your home as determined by an appraiser. As an added bonus, any cash you receive from the refinance is tax free.

Shorten Your Mortgage Loan Term

Another great reason to refinance is to shorten your loan term. Some of the benefits include:

  • • Lower interest rates
  • • Build equity faster over a shortened loan term
  • • Save thousands in interest payments over the life of the loan

Here’s an example of those benefits in action. If you borrow $200,000 with a 30-year mortgage at an interest rate of 4.1 percent, you will spend $118,323 in total interest over the life of the loan. By comparison, if you borrow $200,000 using a 15-year mortgage with an interest rate of 3.4 percent, your total interest drops to $44,475 over the life of the loan.

If you can support the higher monthly mortgage payment, then shortening the loan term adds up to huge savings and helps you become debt-free a lot sooner.

Lower Your Monthly Mortgage Payment

The most common reason to refinance your mortgage is to take advantage of lower interest rates and decrease your monthly payment. Who couldn’t use an extra few hundred dollars a month? But there are some things to keep in mind before you decide to pull the trigger:

  • • Closing Costs — As when you purchased your home, you’ll have closing costs associated with completing a refinance. Make sure you lower your interest rate by at least 1 percent to offset these costs. Ideally, you want to recoup closing costs within a year.
  • • Selling Your Home — If you’re planning on selling your home and moving within 1-3 years, it may not make sense to refinance. You wouldn’t see much savings after closing costs are recouped.
  • • Resetting Your Term — Having a lower monthly payment is great, but you may be starting over with a 30-year mortgage. Doing so is simply spreading the currently principal owed over 30 years more. If you are well into your mortgage, a better option is to consider shortening your loan term. This increases your interest payment savings.
Remove a Co-Borrower from Your Mortgage

Sometimes life happens and a committed relationship ends in separation or divorce. That leaves you and your mortgage in a precarious situation if the other party was on the loan as a co-borrower. The only way to remove a co-borrower from the mortgage is to refinance the loan. Yet, in cases of separation or divorce, you may be required to refinance or even sell the home. You must be able to qualify for the loan using only your income and assets to make it through underwriting.

Are You Eligible for a Mortgage Refinance?

Applying for a refinance is a similar process to when you first purchased your home. No, they don’t go any easier on you even if you have a perfect payment history — you must go through the entire process and underwriting again.

Check Your Credit Score

Make sure your credit score is in good standing:

  • • Make all payments on time, every time.
  • • Keep credit card balances below 30 percent of your credit limit.
  • • Pay all taxes and ensure you have no outstanding bills in collections.

There are many websites that will help you determine your credit score for free before you call your mortgage consultant. However, a mortgage credit pull goes into more detail than a free online report and the scores often differ. Reach out to your lender for a free credit pull to see what changes you need to make to qualify for a refinance.

Maintain Your Employment Status

Have you made any changes to your employment status since purchasing of your home? Switching from one full-time job to another is not a hindrance. Yet, if you’ve started your own company, you may not be eligible for a new mortgage until you have a two-year history of self-employment. Also, if you’ve changed from a salaried position to one that’s commission-based, you’ll need to show a two-year history of income as well. This allows the underwriter to average the two. Make your mortgage lender aware if your employment structure has changed since the last time you met.

Know Your Debt-to-Income Ratio

Your debt-to-income ratio is the amount of monthly payment obligations showing on your credit report divided by your gross monthly income. It’s important to get your DTI as low as possible. Ideally, you want your DTI to reflect no more than 43 percent, but lower is better.

Your DTI will be reevaluated when you refinance. If you’ve made any large purchases, taken out student loans, or even co-signed a loan for a family member, these obligations will be added to the ratio.

Get a Mortgage Refinance Appraisal

The appraisal is one of the most important pieces of a refinance. If for any reason your home’s value has declined, you may not have enough equity to refinance. When the housing market is doing well, this usually isn’t an issue. But there are times when we have seen declines in the housing markets, too. During times of decline it’s difficult to get the appraisal value homeowners need to qualify for a new mortgage loan.

Your home’s condition also comes into question during an appraisal. If there are any glaring problems like a leaking roof or safety concerns spotted by an appraiser, fixing them could be a condition of the underwriter’s final approval. This could also make the refinance more expensive. Here are a few tips to get your home ready for its appraisal:

  • • Repair any major issues around your home. Always repair a leaking roof, loose floorboards, dangling gutters, or a hole in the wall. The underwriter might make fixing these items a condition of closing on your loan.
  • • Do some decluttering. No, the tidiness of your home shouldn’t affect its value. Yet, a tidy home improves the appraiser’s overall impression of your home.
  • • Spend time on your curb appeal. Mow your lawn make sure your yard is free from debris. Check the front doorway for peeling paint; scrape and repaint if necessary. Add some planters with flowers and a fresh, new doormat. First impressions speak volumes!
  • • Do a deep clean. Wipe down walls and cabinets. Don’t forget to vacuum carpets and mop your floors. Dusting also helps, as does cleaning the kitchen and bathrooms to a sparkling shine. If you have any pets, neutralize any pet odors in the home.

This step-by-step refinance guide will familiarize you with the process. Each time you buy a new house or refinance your current mortgage the same protocol must be followed. Income, assets, job history, tax returns, and credit scores are scrutinized by an underwriter. This ensures you continue to meet the mortgage guidelines and lending regulations. This is a new loan and the lender must do their due diligence to comply with all federal lending laws and guidelines.
