Manage Your New Mortgage Loan Payment

You’ve made it through the entire refinance process. It’s time to reap the rewards. Whether you wanted a lower monthly payment or a short loan term, here’s what happens in the days and months following your closing day.

Cash Out Refinance - If you’re using a cash out refinance to consolidate debt or invest in your education, you can expect to receive the funds 3-5 days after closing. The lender must allow at least three days to ensure you don’t want to cancel the refinance.

First Mortgage Payment - Similar to a purchase, you’ll get to skip an entire month before your first payment is due. If you close in June, your first payment is due Aug. 1. If you close in December, you won’t make your first payment until Feb. 1 of the following year.

Escrow Refund - Within 30 days of closing, you should receive a refund check from your previous mortgage servicer. These were the funds being held to pay your property taxes and homeowner’s insurance. When your mortgage lender issued your new mortgage, funds were set aside to start a new escrow account allowing payment for these bills as they come due.

Making Your Payments - You’ll receive payment coupons during your closing with directions for how to make your first mortgage payments. If your mortgage is sold to a new mortgage servicer, then you’ll receive instructions from the new servicer on how and when to make payments to them. Once your servicer is finalized, it’s a good idea to set up automatic drafts for you mortgage payments. This way, you don’t forget to make a payment on time. Late mortgage payments can significantly impact your credit and your ability to borrow money in the future.

Making Extra Payments - If you decided against refinancing to a shorter term due to concerns over affordability, making extra mortgage payments offers you another option. The extra payments go toward the principal balance of your mortgage. An extra few thousand dollars each year cuts years off the life of your loan. For example, on a $480,000 loan, adding an extra $300 payment each month shaves almost six years off the length of a 30-year mortgage, turning it into a 24-year term.
