Should You Sell Before Buying a New Home?

Most home buyers, especially first-time buyers who might have inherited the home they’re currently living in, prefer to sell their current home before purchasing a new one. This “out with the old, in with the new” mantra actually makes a lot of practical sense: sitting on a home is a lot like sitting on unused cash—cash that could be used towards the purchase of a new home.

But a home is also an asset, and assets can appreciate and depreciate, and, if one is clever about it, be used to turn a profit. If you plan to sell your house, you would ideally like to sell for the highest price possible, giving you the money necessary for moving and buying a home.

Sometimes, however, the market is not conducive to selling. If you are attempting to sell your house in a buyer’s market, expect to get shortchanged, because the buyer is king. If you’re selling in a seller’s market, however, and house values are rising, you can expect to walk away with a pleasant, tidy sum. If the current market is a bust, don’t feel pressured to sell. In fact, it’s not necessary to sell your current home before buying a second home. In fact with a moving home mortgage, you don’t have to have any hesitations about moving into a new home while keeping your old one. Then, when market forces have turned in your favor, you can sell your previous home at a much better price.

FAQ’s

Does Pre-approval mean I am guaranteed to get a loan?

Not necessarily. Pre-approval is a thorough investigation of your application for a mortgage loan. The result (if successful) will be a letter that indicates you are qualified for a mortgage at the amount you have requested. That doesn't always mean you will get final approval for the mortgage. Lots of other factors impact the mortgage approval process—the value of the home is key, as well as potential changes to your credit or employment status between pre-approval and final determination.

How can I improve my chances of getting approved?

Great question. One smart move is to check your credit reports before you start the application. If there is any incorrect information, you can begin the process of getting it resolved. Also, don’t open or close any credit accounts if you can avoid it—those actions can cause a temporary decrease in your score. The other thing that helps is to give us as much information as possible—fill out everything completely.

What are points on a loan?

A point on a loan is a price the borrower pays to lower the interest rate. A lender may offer a loan with a specific interest rate and a one-point fee. The "point" equals one percent of the loan total, paid in advance. It might make sense to pay the point to bring down the interest rate, especially if you plan to remain in the home over a long period.

Are interest rates and points the only things to consider when choosing a lender?

No. There are numerous factors to evaluate when choosing your lender. Reputation is essential, and the costs of applying and completing the mortgage process. Also, look out for questionable practices like pre-payment penalties, which means the lender will charge you a fee if you pay the loan off early or refinance.

How do I get a rate lock?

A rate lock promises that the lender can complete the processing of your loan with the interest rate and points available on a particular day. The "lock" is valid for a period of time—usually 30 or 60 days. Sometimes you can lock in the rate just by asking while you navigate through the approval process. Depending on the loan program, you might have to pay a fee to guarantee the rate.
