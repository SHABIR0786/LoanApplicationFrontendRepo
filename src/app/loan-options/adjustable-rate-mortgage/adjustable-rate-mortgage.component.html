Is an adjustable rate
mortgage loan right for me

Adjustable rate mortgages offer advantages for some borrowers, but they have higher risks than fixed rate mortgages. ARMs, as they are commonly referred to, have a low interest rate for a set period, and then the rate can rise or fall with market rates.

The period depends on the terms of your loan, with common introductory rates locked in for anywhere from three to ten years. Some ARMs have limits on how much the rate can rise per year, but others do not. An ARM might make sense for you if you are planning to move in a few years. Keep in mind that if you owe more than the house is worth (referred to as being underwater) you may not be able to do so.

What are the advantages of
an adjustable rate loan

An Adjustable Rate Mortgage will allow you to obtain a mortgage with a lower rate than what is typically available with a 15 or 30 year fixed rate loan, in exchange for accepting the risk of increases at a later date.

This makes sense for the lender because they aren’t locked into a mortgage rate for 15 or 30 years, and it makes sense for the borrower because you can take advantage of a low starter rate. It’s important to be aware that the rate can rise after the initial period, and your payment may increase substantially.

How do I qualify for
an adjustable rate loan

The qualifying process is the same, whether the loan has a fixed rate of interest or an adjustable rate. In fact, some borrowers may find it easier to qualify for an ARM, since the initial payment is lower.

A lower payment reduces the expense ratio, and this can expand the loan amount for which you may qualify.

Why choose an Adjustable Rate Mortgage (ARM) loan?

  • Choose an ARM if you need a lower interest rate to start.
  • Choose an ARM if you don’t plan to remain in the home for a long period.
  • Choose an ARM if you can absorb an increase in the payment if necessary.

How Does Mortgage Insurance Work

You’ll have to pay primary mortgage insurance (PMI) if your down payment is less than 20%.

  • Mortgage insurance is required on almost every new loan with a down payment of less than twenty percent.
  • Mortgage insurance protects the lender from losses, and this allows them to offer loans they might not otherwise.
  • Most conventional loans qualify for private mortgage insurance, paid monthly along with the principal and interest.
  • In some cases with a conventional loan, you can request to have the mortgage insurance dropped when your equity in the loan reaches twenty percent, but in other cases you may need to refinance the loan.
  • FHA loans have mortgage insurance that you pay to the Federal Housing Administration. For these loans there is a fee at the loan closing plus a monthly charge.
  • FHA loans must maintain the mortgage insurance, so to stop paying the MI, you need to refinance into a conventional loan type.
  • VA (Veteran’s Administration) loans don’t need mortgage insurance, because the VA guarantees the loan for you. But there is an upfront funding fee that varies depending on several factors, including:
  • Your service type
  • Your down payment
  • Whether you are buying or refinancing
  • Whether you are disabled
  • If this is your first VA loan
